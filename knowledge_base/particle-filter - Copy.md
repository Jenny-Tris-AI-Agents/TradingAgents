### 1. Strategy Overview: Hedge Fund Replication
The core strategy is **Dynamic Factor Replication**. Instead of picking individual stocks, the strategy attempts to "clone" the performance of hedge funds (the target) by identifying their time-varying exposures to liquid market factors (Alternative Beta).

### 2. Strategy Logic & Mathematical Framework
The strategy is framed as a **State-Space Tracking Problem**. The logic assumes that hedge fund returns are a function of dynamic exposures ($\beta$) to specific market factors.

#### A. The Observation Equation (Linear Case)
The return of the hedge fund at time $t$ ($R_t$) is modeled as:
$$R_t = \beta_t^T F_t + \epsilon_t$$
Where:
*   $F_t$: A vector of liquid factor returns (e.g., S&P 500, VIX, Bond indices).
*   $\beta_t$: The time-varying exposure (state vector) to these factors.
*   $\epsilon_t \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$: The idiosyncratic return or "tracking error."

#### B. The Transition Equation (State Evolution)
The manager’s tactical asset allocation (TAA) is assumed to follow a random walk or a first-order autoregressive process, allowing the weights to evolve over time:
$$\beta_t = \beta_{t-1} + \eta_t$$
Where $\eta_t \sim N(0, Q)$ represents the "reactivity" of the manager.

### 3. Entry and Exit Signals (Tracking Logic)
In a replication strategy, "Entry" and "Exit" are not binary signals but **continuous rebalancing signals** generated by Bayesian filters:

*   **Kalman Filter (Linear Tracking):** Used when factor exposures are assumed to be linear. The "signal" is the posterior mean of $\beta_t$ given the observed returns $R_{1:t}$.
*   **Particle Filter (Non-Linear Tracking):** Used to capture non-linear exposures (like option-like payoffs). It uses a set of "particles" (Monte Carlo simulations) to represent the probability distribution of the weights.
    *   **Signal Logic:** The strategy rebalances the replication portfolio to match the expected $\beta_t$ calculated from the particles:
    $$\hat{\beta}_t = \sum_{i=1}^N w_t^i \beta_t^i$$
    Where $w_t^i$ are the importance weights of the particles.

### 4. VIX Correlation & Alternative Beta
The document identifies the **VIX** as a critical factor in capturing "Alternative Beta."
*   **Volatility as a Factor:** Hedge funds often exhibit non-linear correlation with market volatility. The strategy uses VIX futures or volatility indices to replicate the "short-vol" profile common in many hedge fund strategies (e.g., Merger Arbitrage or Carry trades).
*   **Non-Linearity:** The paper notes that standard OLS fails to capture the "convexity" of hedge fund returns. By using a **Particle Filter**, the strategy can track the correlation between the fund's returns and VIX spikes, effectively replicating the fund's tail-risk hedging behavior.

### 5. Strategy Implementation Logic
1.  **Factor Selection:** Identify liquid proxies (S&P 500, VIX, GSCI, US Dollar Index).
2.  **State Estimation:** Use a **Particle Filter** to estimate the hidden state $\beta_t$ (the current "bet" of the hedge fund industry).
3.  **Portfolio Construction:**
    *   **Core:** The replicated beta (liquid portion).
    *   **Satellite:** Illiquid or "pure alpha" components that cannot be replicated.
4.  **Rebalancing:** Re-weight the factor portfolio as the Bayesian filter updates the posterior distribution of $\beta$ based on the most recent realized returns.

### 6. Key Formula for Particle Filtering (Sequential Importance Sampling)
To update the weights (signals) of the replication factors:
$$w_t^i \propto w_{t-1}^i \frac{p(R_t | \beta_t^i) p(\beta_t^i | \beta_{t-1}^i)}{q(\beta_t^i | \beta_{t-1}^i, R_t)}$$
This allows the strategy to react faster to market regime shifts than traditional rolling-window OLS regressions.
